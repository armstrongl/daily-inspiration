### When punishment helps cooperation

Two decades and over 2,500 papers studying whether punishment helps cooperation in public goods games couldn't resolve the question, because each study varied one factor at a time. Almaatouq, Alsobay, and colleagues varied 14 factors simultaneously across 360 experimental conditions with 7,100 participants and found that punishment's effect on collective welfare swings from a 43% improvement to a 44% reduction depending on the specific combination of context. The single most decisive factor turned out to be whether participants could communicate — three times more influential than anything else studied.

[Read the source](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.aeb5280)

*Chosen because it reveals that a question 2,500 papers couldn't answer was an artifact of how the question was being asked, not its inherent difficulty.*

### A galaxy that aged too fast

Galaxy XMM-VID1-2075 contains several times more stars than the Milky Way, had already stopped forming new ones, and shows no rotation at all — a state previously observed only in the most massive galaxies near us, the ones that have had billions of years of mergers to settle into stillness. This galaxy reached that state when the universe was less than two billion years old. One possible explanation: a single head-on collision between two galaxies spinning in opposite directions.

[Read the source](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41550-026-02855-0)

*Chosen because it compresses a timeline that should span billions of years into a fraction of that, with a possible mechanism that's almost elegant in its simplicity.*

### Mapping the unmapped fungal networks

Justin Stewart at SPUN compiled three billion DNA sequences and 25,000 soil samples into the first global assessment of where arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi have been sequenced — and found that over 70% of Earth's ecosystems have no data at all. These are the fungal networks that route roughly a billion tons of carbon from plants to soil each year, and most of the planet's ecosystems remain unmapped for them. The study reads less as a finding and more as an open invitation to look.

[Read the source](https://doi.org/10.1093/femsle/fnaf055)

*Chosen because it reframes every patch of soil as unmapped territory for a system that moves a billion tons of carbon annually.*
