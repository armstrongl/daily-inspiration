A routine vaccine for a painful skin rash may be one of the closest things we have to dementia prevention. Stanford researcher Pascal Geldsetzer spotted a natural experiment in Wales: when the shingles vaccine rolled out on September 1, 2013, anyone who was 79 could get it, but those who had turned 80 even a week earlier were permanently ineligible. That arbitrary cutoff created something close to a randomized trial across 280,000 people. Over seven years, those who received the vaccine were 20% less likely to be diagnosed with dementia — and a follow-up study in Cell found the vaccine also slowed progression in people who already had the disease. [Source](https://med.stanford.edu/news/all-news/2025/03/shingles-vaccination-dementia.html)
*Chosen because it upends the assumption that dementia and infectious disease are separate problems, using a study design that sidesteps the usual "healthy user" bias.*

For at least 500 years, Babylonian children sat in school and memorized a 250-line hymn celebrating their city, its temples, its priestesses, and the way the Euphrates greened the fields each spring. Then the hymn vanished. In 2025, LMU Assyriologist Enrique Jiménez, working with the University of Baghdad to digitize every known cuneiform fragment, used AI to match 30 scattered clay tablet fragments across collections worldwide and reconstructed the complete text — a poem that describes urban life, the roles of women, and the treatment of foreigners in a city that was once the largest on Earth. [Source](https://scitechdaily.com/researchers-discover-lost-3000-year-old-babylonian-hymn/)
*Chosen because a text memorized by generations of children and then lost for a millennium is a reminder of how much of the human record exists in fragments we haven't yet assembled.*

Iris Murdoch argued that moral progress doesn't come from dramatic moments of choice, the existentialist fork in the road. It comes from something quieter: the sustained practice of paying careful, just attention to other people. Her example is a mother-in-law who finds her daughter-in-law "unpolished" and "juvenile." The mother-in-law's outward behavior was always perfectly courteous — the change happens entirely in how she looks, until she comes to see "not vulgar but refreshingly simple, not undignified but spontaneous." What if there's one person or situation you've been viewing through a fixed lens that might shift under more careful, generous attention? [Source](https://eganphilosophy.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/Murdoch-The-Sovereignty-of-Good-Over-Other-Concepts.pdf)
*Chosen because it offers a concrete, daily-scale practice for moral growth that requires no special resources — only the willingness to look more carefully.*
