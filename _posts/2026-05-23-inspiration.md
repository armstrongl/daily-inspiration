Construction has a reputation as a laggard, slow to adopt new methods. Brian Potter spent four and a half years and 186 essays investigating this and found the reputation is wrong: innovations spread through construction at roughly the same rate as through car manufacturing or agriculture. The deeper puzzle is that despite adopting innovations at a normal pace, construction costs in the US have never fallen for as far back as we have data — over 150 years. Individual tasks haven't gotten cheaper since at least the 1950s, and bricks haven't gotten cheaper since the mid-19th century, despite massive improvements in brickmaking technology. [Source](https://www.construction-physics.com/p/50-things-ive-learned-writing-construction)
*Chosen because it dismantles two interlocking assumptions simultaneously: that construction is uniquely resistant to change, and that innovation should make things cheaper.*

In early 2026, scientists aboard the research vessel Falkor (too) sent ROV SuBastian into the deep waters off Argentina expecting to map cold seeps and found an ecosystem beyond what they'd imagined: 28 probable new species, a Bathelia candida coral reef nearly the size of Vatican City, and — at 3,890 meters — the country's first recorded deep-sea whale fall, its bones sustaining a temporary city of sharks and crabs. Among the finds, a bus-length phantom jellyfish (Stygiomedusa gigantea) drifting at 250 meters with juvenile fish sheltering around its bell. Among the artifacts, a near-pristine VHS tape. [Source](https://www.popsci.com/environment/new-deep-sea-species-argentina/)
*Chosen because the density of unknown life at a single site makes the 99.999% of unexplored seafloor feel vertiginous.*

Cognitive scientist Tommy Blanchard, drawing on Kennett and Faust's network analysis, found that highly creative people don't think harder — they have denser webs of associations between concepts in semantic memory, meaning fewer leaps are needed to reach a non-obvious connection. The difference isn't raw talent but structural: the more domains you've engaged with, the more bridges exist between seemingly unrelated ideas. One low-cost way to build those bridges — when you encounter a striking idea in one field, spend five minutes asking what it would look like transplanted into a completely different one. [Source](https://cognitivewonderland.substack.com/p/the-cognitive-mechanics-of-creativity)
*Chosen because it converts a structural finding about creative cognition into a practice small enough to try today.*
