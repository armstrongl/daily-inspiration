### Shingles vaccine and dementia prevention

A routine vaccine for a painful skin rash may be one of the closest things we have to dementia prevention. Stanford researcher Pascal Geldsetzer spotted a natural experiment in Wales: when the shingles vaccine rolled out on September 1, 2013, anyone who was 79 could get it, but those who had turned 80 even a week earlier were permanently ineligible. That arbitrary cutoff created something close to a randomized trial across 280,000 people. Over seven years, those who received the vaccine were 20% less likely to be diagnosed with dementia — and a follow-up study in Cell found the vaccine also slowed progression in people who already had the disease.

[Read the source](https://med.stanford.edu/news/all-news/2025/03/shingles-vaccination-dementia.html)

*Chosen because it upends the assumption that dementia and infectious disease are separate problems, using a study design that sidesteps the usual "healthy user" bias.*

### A lost Babylonian hymn reconstructed

For at least 500 years, Babylonian children sat in school and memorized a 250-line hymn celebrating their city, its temples, its priestesses, and the way the Euphrates greened the fields each spring. Then the hymn vanished. In 2025, LMU Assyriologist Enrique Jiménez, working with the University of Baghdad to digitize every known cuneiform fragment, used AI to match 30 scattered clay tablet fragments across collections worldwide and reconstructed the complete text — a poem that describes urban life, the roles of women, and the treatment of foreigners in a city that was once the largest on Earth.

[Read the source](https://scitechdaily.com/researchers-discover-lost-3000-year-old-babylonian-hymn/)

*Chosen because a text memorized by generations of children and then lost for a millennium is a reminder of how much of the human record exists in fragments we haven't yet assembled.*

### Moral progress through attention

Iris Murdoch argued that moral progress doesn't come from dramatic moments of choice, the existentialist fork in the road. It comes from something quieter: the sustained practice of paying careful, just attention to other people. Her example is a mother-in-law who finds her daughter-in-law "unpolished" and "juvenile." The mother-in-law's outward behavior was always perfectly courteous — the change happens entirely in how she looks, until she comes to see "not vulgar but refreshingly simple, not undignified but spontaneous." What if there's one person or situation you've been viewing through a fixed lens that might shift under more careful, generous attention?

[Read the source](https://eganphilosophy.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/05/Murdoch-The-Sovereignty-of-Good-Over-Other-Concepts.pdf)

*Chosen because it offers a concrete, daily-scale practice for moral growth that requires no special resources — only the willingness to look more carefully.*

### Construction costs never fall

Construction has a reputation as a laggard, slow to adopt new methods. Brian Potter spent four and a half years and 186 essays investigating this and found the reputation is wrong: innovations spread through construction at roughly the same rate as through car manufacturing or agriculture. The deeper puzzle is that despite adopting innovations at a normal pace, construction costs in the US have never fallen for as far back as we have data — over 150 years. Individual tasks haven't gotten cheaper since at least the 1950s, and bricks haven't gotten cheaper since the mid-19th century, despite massive improvements in brickmaking technology.

[Read the source](https://www.construction-physics.com/p/50-things-ive-learned-writing-construction)

*Chosen because it dismantles two interlocking assumptions simultaneously: that construction is uniquely resistant to change, and that innovation should make things cheaper.*

### 28 new species off Argentina

In early 2026, scientists aboard the research vessel Falkor (too) sent ROV SuBastian into the deep waters off Argentina expecting to map cold seeps and found an ecosystem beyond what they'd imagined: 28 probable new species, a Bathelia candida coral reef nearly the size of Vatican City, and — at 3,890 meters — the country's first recorded deep-sea whale fall, its bones sustaining a temporary city of sharks and crabs. Among the finds, a bus-length phantom jellyfish (Stygiomedusa gigantea) drifting at 250 meters with juvenile fish sheltering around its bell. Among the artifacts, a near-pristine VHS tape.

[Read the source](https://www.popsci.com/environment/new-deep-sea-species-argentina/)

*Chosen because the density of unknown life at a single site makes the 99.999% of unexplored seafloor feel vertiginous.*

### Creative cognition is structural, not talent

Cognitive scientist Tommy Blanchard, drawing on Kennett and Faust's network analysis, found that highly creative people don't think harder — they have denser webs of associations between concepts in semantic memory, meaning fewer leaps are needed to reach a non-obvious connection. The difference isn't raw talent but structural: the more domains you've engaged with, the more bridges exist between seemingly unrelated ideas. One low-cost way to build those bridges — when you encounter a striking idea in one field, spend five minutes asking what it would look like transplanted into a completely different one.

*Chosen because it converts a structural finding about creative cognition into a practice small enough to try today.*
