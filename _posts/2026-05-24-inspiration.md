### Game design is prediction management



---

### Raph Koster, designer of Ultima Online and author of

Raph Koster, designer of Ultima Online and author of A Theory of Fun, argues that the entire field of game design reduces to one axis: managing uncertainty. Games are prediction machines that pose problems whose outcomes are indeterminate, and fun emerges specifically when you make progress reducing that indeterminacy. A singular puzzle with one answer isn't a game; it becomes one only when uncertainty compounds across what Koster calls a "progression spiral." The implication extends well beyond games: any domain where you sequence uncertainty for someone else — teaching, product design, conversation — is structurally doing game design.

[Read the source](https://www.raphkoster.com/2025/11/03/game-design-is-simple-actually/)

*Chosen because it collapses an intimidatingly broad field into a single explanatory axis, reframing "fun" from a subjective feeling into a measurable cognitive event.*

### Earth as a four-billion-year readable archive



---

### Geologist Marcia Bjornerud proposes

Geologist Marcia Bjornerud proposes that rocks are best understood not as nouns but as verbs — each one signifying an event or process rather than a static object. Earth has compressed four billion years of planetary history into readable strata, a feat no neighbor has matched: Venus periodically lacquers itself in lava, the Moon went silent, and Mars stopped recording in childhood. We live on the only planet that both continuously generates new rock and makes its deep archives accessible from the surface — through the counterintuitive mechanism that erosion, which destroys rocks, is also what exposes them for reading.

[Read the source](https://emergencemagazine.org/essay/wrinkled-time/)

*Chosen because it reframes the ground beneath your feet as an open library containing billions of years of compressed planetary autobiography.*

### The upside-down drawing shift



---

### When you present your brain with an inverted

Betty Edwards discovered that when you present your brain with an inverted image to draw, your verbal naming system disengages — it can't efficiently label upside-down features — and your visual-spatial perception takes over. The result is that people who "can't draw" suddenly produce accurate, detailed renderings because they're finally seeing edges and spatial relationships rather than symbols their brain has substituted for observation. You might try this in five minutes with any printed image flipped upside down: draw the lines you actually see rather than what you know should be there, and notice the perceptual shift when naming stops.

[Read the source](https://www.drawright.com/theory)

*Chosen because it offers an immediately testable five-minute exercise that demonstrates how much of ordinary seeing is actually symbolic substitution.*

### Polarization comes from contact, not isolation



---

### A study published this month in Sociological Science found

A study published this month in Sociological Science found that online polarization isn't driven by echo chambers walling people off from opposing views. Keuchenius, Törnberg, and Uitermark built signed network models of Twitter/X debates, distinguishing hostile from supportive interactions, and found that digital media polarize not by keeping groups apart but by bringing them into antagonistic contact. The mechanism is conflict across group lines, not insulation within them.

[Read the source](https://sociologicalscience.com/articles-v13-22-565/)

*Chosen because it inverts the dominant "filter bubble" mental model: the problem isn't that we never encounter opposing views, but that we encounter them as enemies.*

### Ancient stone corridors that tune their own instruments



---

### The architecture acoustically couples with the instruments

At Chavín de Huántar in the Peruvian Andes, Stanford researchers played 3,000-year-old conch shell horns inside the ceremonial center's labyrinthine stone corridors and found that the architecture acoustically couples with the instruments, pulling their tones "into tune" with the building's resonant frequencies. Players reported feeling an external force guiding their shells, and when two performers sounded together, wave interference between instruments and stone produced a low warbling that present-day listeners mistook for a performance technique.

[Read the source](https://ccrma.stanford.edu/groups/chavin/ASA2014.html)

*Chosen because it reveals ancient engineering sophistication that exceeds modern expectations — architecture designed to become part of the instrument.*

### The Ben Franklin method for knowledge work



---

### Andy Matuschak observes that knowledge workers are surprisingly

Andy Matuschak observes that knowledge workers are surprisingly unserious about honing fundamental skills compared to athletes or musicians. Ben Franklin practiced writing by reading a compelling essay, setting it aside, reconstructing it from memory, and then studying the differences between his version and the original. What fundamental skill in your own work might benefit from that kind of deliberate, comparative practice — not more repetition, but structured attention to the gap between what you produce and what excellence looks like?

*Chosen because it offers a specific, imitable technique rather than generic advice about "practice more."*
