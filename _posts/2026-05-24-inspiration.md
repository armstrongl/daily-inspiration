### Polarization comes from contact, not isolation



---

### A study published this month in Sociological Science found

A study published this month in Sociological Science found that online polarization isn't driven by echo chambers walling people off from opposing views. Keuchenius, Törnberg, and Uitermark built signed network models of Twitter/X debates, distinguishing hostile from supportive interactions, and found that digital media polarize not by keeping groups apart but by bringing them into antagonistic contact. The mechanism is conflict across group lines, not insulation within them.

[Read the source](https://sociologicalscience.com/articles-v13-22-565/)

*Chosen because it inverts the dominant "filter bubble" mental model: the problem isn't that we never encounter opposing views, but that we encounter them as enemies.*

### Ancient stone corridors that tune their own instruments



---

### The architecture acoustically couples with the instruments

At Chavín de Huántar in the Peruvian Andes, Stanford researchers played 3,000-year-old conch shell horns inside the ceremonial center's labyrinthine stone corridors and found that the architecture acoustically couples with the instruments, pulling their tones "into tune" with the building's resonant frequencies. Players reported feeling an external force guiding their shells, and when two performers sounded together, wave interference between instruments and stone produced a low warbling that present-day listeners mistook for a performance technique.

[Read the source](https://ccrma.stanford.edu/groups/chavin/ASA2014.html)

*Chosen because it reveals ancient engineering sophistication that exceeds modern expectations — architecture designed to become part of the instrument.*

### The Ben Franklin method for knowledge work



---

### Andy Matuschak observes that knowledge workers are surprisingly

Andy Matuschak observes that knowledge workers are surprisingly unserious about honing fundamental skills compared to athletes or musicians. Ben Franklin practiced writing by reading a compelling essay, setting it aside, reconstructing it from memory, and then studying the differences between his version and the original. What fundamental skill in your own work might benefit from that kind of deliberate, comparative practice — not more repetition, but structured attention to the gap between what you produce and what excellence looks like?

[Read the source](https://notes.andymatuschak.org/zBmSSpM1WfFDehxNCBcqSZp?stackedNotes=zMX9Lfuz8sGfDUivWZcyWT)

*Chosen because it offers a specific, imitable technique rather than generic advice about "practice more."*
