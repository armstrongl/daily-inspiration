### A tabletop route to particle-collider physics

The Wiedemann-Franz law states that electrical and thermal conductivity in metals rise and fall together — a principle physicists have treated as foundational for over 170 years. At the Indian Institute of Science, a team created ultraclean graphene and measured the two properties moving in opposite directions, deviating from the law by a factor of 200. At graphene's Dirac point, electrons abandon individual particle behavior and flow collectively as a nearly frictionless liquid — a "Dirac fluid" that mimics quark-gluon plasma, the exotic state normally produced only in particle colliders. A single sheet of carbon atoms now offers a tabletop route into physics previously confined to extreme-energy facilities.

### Life below nine thousand meters

In the Japan, Ryukyu, and Izu-Ogasawara trenches, a crewed submersible and baited landers cataloged over 100 distinct life forms between 4,534 and 9,775 meters down. At the bottom of the Izu-Ogasawara Trench, carnivorous sponges from the Cladorhizidae family were recorded at hadal depths for the first time — predatory organisms thriving under pressures that collapse most equipment. A snailfish was filmed actively feeding at 8,336 meters, setting a new record for the deepest in-situ vertebrate observation. And twice, at depths exceeding 9,000 meters, cameras captured an organism so unlike anything in existing taxonomy that global experts could not place it in a phylum.

[Read the source](https://dx.doi.org/10.3897/BDJ.14.e182172)

*Chosen because the specific details — carnivorous sponges at record depths, a vertebrate feeding 8 kilometers down, an organism no taxonomist on Earth can classify — make the scale of the unknown ocean viscerally concrete.*

### Diagnosing where your learning loop breaks

A reframe grounded in ["Learning as closing feedback loops"](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/cfBMp8WvDvzRAWCPw/learning-as-closing-feedback-loops): Most learning advice — deliberate practice, spaced repetition, getting a mentor — can be understood as fixing a specific broken link in a three-step feedback loop: make an attempt, observe the outcome, then reflect and adjust. The essay catalogs where each link typically snaps: you practice without observing (playing a song 100 times on autopilot), you observe but can't interpret the signal (getting audience applause but not knowing what worked), or you reflect but don't change your next attempt (recognizing you underestimate deadlines, then estimating the same way). One diagnostic worth trying with any skill you're currently working on: which of those three links is the weakest right now, and what's one specific change that would close the gap?

[Read the source](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/cfBMp8WvDvzRAWCPw/learning-as-closing-feedback-loops)

*Chosen because it replaces vague "practice better" advice with a specific diagnostic question applicable to any skill, testable tomorrow.*
