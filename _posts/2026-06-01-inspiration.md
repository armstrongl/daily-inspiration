### Five million bees beneath a cemetery

Rachel Fordyce used to walk through an Ithaca cemetery on her way to work at a Cornell entomology lab to save on parking. One spring she noticed bees everywhere, brought a jar to her boss, and they discovered 5.5 million ground-nesting bees packed into 1.5 acres of undisturbed sandy soil, one of the largest aggregations ever documented. The species, Andrena regularis, has likely been there for over a century, emerging each April timed to the apple bloom. Seventy-five percent of all bee species share its solitary, ground-nesting lifestyle, the most common way of being a bee and the one we never think about.

### The bacteria inside every fish

Bony fish drink seawater to stay hydrated and excrete the excess calcium as solid mineral pellets, a process scientists assumed the fish handled alone. A team at the University of Miami found that a Vibrio bacterium living in the gut of the Gulf toadfish carries the genetic machinery for altering local pH and triggering calcium carbonate crystallization. What looked like a fish-only process turned out to be a symbiosis. Marine fish collectively produce a significant fraction of the ocean's calcium carbonate, one of the planet's primary carbon sinks. The chemistry of the global ocean may depend, in part, on bacteria inside individual fish.

[Read the source](https://journals.plos.org/plosbiology/article?id=10.1371/journal.pbio.3003764)

*Chosen because it telescopes from a single bacterium inside a single fish to the chemistry of the entire ocean.*

### A problem-solving framework hidden in prayer

Cognitive scientist Eleanor Schille-Hudson watched Christians pray and found they were unknowingly running mathematician George Polya's four-step problem-solving framework: articulate the problem, make a plan, execute, reflect. In a controlled experiment with 100 participants, prayer and thinking aloud were equally good at structuring problems, but prayer produced significantly more relief and optimism because it adds distraction reduction, externalized speech, and the deliberate act of setting down what you cannot control. The technique extracts from prayer without requiring belief: close your eyes, name the problem aloud, plan the parts within your control, and hand the rest off. You might try this sequence the next time a problem feels stuck.
