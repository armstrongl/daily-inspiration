### The reaction that worked better without its catalyst

David Vahey, a Cambridge PhD researcher, pulled the photocatalyst out of a reaction as a routine control test and the reaction worked better without it. The result, published in Nature Synthesis, inverts nearly 150 years of Friedel-Crafts chemistry: where the classic reaction demands harsh metals and extreme conditions to forge carbon-carbon bonds, this version uses an LED lamp at room temperature and triggers a self-sustaining chain process. Drug chemists can now make precise structural tweaks at the final stage of development instead of rebuilding molecules from scratch.

### A thousand years of homework on broken pottery

Archaeologists from Cairo and Tübingen have now recovered 43,000 inscribed pottery fragments at Athribis in Upper Egypt, the largest collection from any single Egyptian site, surpassing the famous workers' village at Deir el-Medina. Written in six scripts and languages, the shards span more than a thousand years of daily life: tax receipts in Demotic, students' homework in Greek, delivery orders, administrative lists, and over 130 astrological texts mapping zodiac signs. The cheapest writing surface available turned out to be the most durable record of ordinary existence.

*Chosen because a thousand years of mundane paperwork on disposable pottery, surpassing monumental excavation sites, reframes what it takes to survive deep time.*

### The practice schedule that feels worse and works better

Thomas Mathias and Andrew Goldman gave twenty advanced violinists the same excerpts to learn under three schedules: one piece at a time, gradually mixing, or randomly switching between all pieces every few minutes. The random-switching group felt they were learning less during practice but outperformed the others on retention tests. Motor learning researchers call this contextual interference, and it appears across domains from surgery to mathematics: struggle during practice builds more durable retrieval paths. What might happen if you tried rotating between three related problems every few minutes in your next learning session, instead of finishing one before starting the next?

*Chosen because it offers a concrete, testable inversion of the common "practice until mastery" instinct, from a domain most people would not consult for learning advice.*
