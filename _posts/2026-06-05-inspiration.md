### The microbiome ratio nobody ever measured

You have probably read that your body carries ten bacterial cells for every human one. When Ron Sender, Shai Fuchs, and Ron Milo went looking for the source of that figure, they found it had never actually been measured. Their own tally came to roughly 38 trillion bacteria against 30 trillion human cells, closer to one to one than ten to one. A number that shaped a decade of microbiome writing turned out to be a guess that kept getting repeated.

[Read the source](https://journals.plos.org/plosbiology/article?id=10.1371/journal.pbio.1002533)

*Chosen because it shows how one uninspected estimate can harden into a fact that an entire field repeats.*

### The longest nonstop flight ever recorded

A four-month-old bar-tailed godwit known as B6 left Alaska and did not touch down again until Tasmania, 8,425 miles and eleven days later. It crossed the open Pacific without once stopping to feed, drink, or rest, flapping the entire way rather than gliding. The researchers who tracked it call the flight the longest nonstop journey ever documented for any animal.

[Read the source](https://www.usgs.gov/centers/alaska-science-center/news/juvenile-bar-tailed-godwit-b6-sets-world-record)

*Chosen because the endurance of one young bird stretches the ordinary sense of what a living body can do.*

### Every repair becomes a method of inquiry

Scott Gilbertson taught himself to trace the wiring behind a van's dashboard with a multimeter, and later found that the same habit, following the circuit and testing each point, let him revive a dead fridge while anchored two days' sail from the nearest repair shop. His claim is modest and concrete: almost any repair you take on yourself becomes a method of inquiry that carries over to the next broken thing. The next time something quits on you, you might try to diagnose it yourself before replacing it, and watch what the attempt teaches you.

*Chosen because it locates real agency in one small repair rather than a sweeping life change.*
