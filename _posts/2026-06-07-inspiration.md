### The Dunning-Kruger effect may be autocorrelation

Much of the Dunning-Kruger effect, the popular claim that the least skilled people are the most overconfident about their own ability, may be a statistical artifact. The political economist Blair Fix generated pure random data, with no real relationship between skill and self-assessment, then graphed it exactly the way the original 1999 study did, and the signature rising-then-flat pattern appeared anyway. The effect is partly built into the method, which compares people's test scores against a number that already contains those same scores.

### Every view of humanity's future is wild

The galaxy is already about 13 billion years old and, by Holden Karnofsky's reckoning, could remain capable of supporting life for tens of billions of years more. Set against that span, the few centuries in which humans built almost all of their technology and economic growth occupy a stretch of time too thin to see on the chart. In a 2021 essay he works through the possibilities, that our descendants spread across the stars soon, that they manage it only much later, or that they never do, and shows that each one still places us at an unusually decisive moment, which is why he calls every available view of humanity's future a wild one.

*Chosen because it conveys the scale of cosmic time by showing that no version of the story, fast, slow, or never, can make our own moment look ordinary.*

### Tiny experiments instead of rigid goals

When the neuroscientist Anne-Laure Le Cunff felt stuck, she set aside fixed goals in favor of what she calls tiny experiments: one specific action, committed to for a set stretch of time, written in the form "I will [action] for [duration]." A man she worked with had drifted out of touch with old friends and kept freezing because he wanted to send the perfect message, so he tried a plain version instead, "I will message one friend every week for four weeks," and found his way back into those friendships. Because an experiment has no pass or fail, only something you learn, you might take whatever you have been circling lately, pick one small action and a short window, and treat the coming week as data rather than a test.
