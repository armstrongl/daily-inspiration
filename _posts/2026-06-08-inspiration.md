### The work that leaves no artifact

A reframe grounded in Aristotle's Nicomachean Ethics. Aristotle split human activity into three irreducible kinds: poiesis, the making of a thing that outlasts the work, the way a chair outlasts the carpentry; praxis, acting where the doing is its own point, as in friendship or raising a child; and theoria, understanding something for its own sake. Industrial life quietly folded all three into the first and trained us to read our worth as output, which is why having a skill automated can feel like having a self erased. But two of the three kinds were never outputs to begin with: the doing and the understanding that change who you are leave no artifact behind, and that is exactly why they cannot be handed to a machine.

[Read the source](http://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/nicomachaen.html)

*Chosen because it turns the fear of being automated into a precise distinction: a machine can take what you make, not the doing and understanding that have no output.*

### A mind that arose twice

In 1997, at the Seattle Aquarium, a young giant Pacific octopus took a weighted pill bottle, jetted water at it from the siphon beside her head, and waited for the tank's current to push it back, then did it again, sixteen times, for no reason her keepers could name except the doing of it. Octopuses and humans last shared an ancestor around 670 million years ago, long before anything we would recognize as a brain. If what she was doing counts as play, then play, and the kind of restless mind that reaches for it, has arisen on this planet at least twice, by wholly separate roads.

*Chosen because the awe sits in one concrete scene, a single octopus and a pill bottle, with 670 million years doing the rest.*

### Three prompts that turn looking into seeing

The naturalist John Muir Laws carries a journal into the field that he says he needs more than his binoculars, and his whole method comes down to three phrases he says out loud at whatever he is looking at: "I notice...", "I wonder...", and "It reminds me of...". The first slows you into real observation, the second turns the observation into a question, and the third reaches for a connection to something you already know. You might try it tomorrow on something unremarkable, a street tree or the skin of foam on a coffee, with a pen, two minutes, and no need to draw well. What do you actually see once you make yourself say it out loud?

[Read the source](https://baynature.org/article/nature-journaling-john-muir-laws-and-the-art-of-slowing-down-sitting-still-and-paying-attention/)

*Chosen because it is a two-minute practice with a visible next step, not a system to adopt.*
