### The cost disease behind rising prices

A college seminar, a doctor's visit, and a live string quartet keep getting more expensive, and the cause isn't waste or greed. Working through William Baumol's central paper, the economist Dietrich Vollrath lays out the mechanism: a horn quintet needs the same player-hours it did two centuries ago, since no one wants the half-hour piece performed in twelve minutes, while the factories and software around it kept getting more productive and pulling wages up everywhere. The services that resist automation grow pricier precisely because everything else got cheaper to make.

### Most of Earth's microbial life is underground

Most of Earth's microbial life isn't on the surface at all. Around 85 percent of the planet's microbial biomass sits in dark rock beneath your feet, kilometers down and cut off from sunlight. In a 2025 review, Emeline Vidal and colleagues describe cells down there subsisting on energy fluxes of tens to hundreds of zeptowatts, millions of times less power than a lab-grown E. coli cell, so faint that a single cell may divide just once in hundreds to thousands of years. It is a biosphere that keeps geological time, mostly out of our sight.

*Chosen because it relocates most of life to a place we never picture and stretches a single cell's lifespan to the scale of geology.*

### Spaced repetition as a tool for understanding

The physicist Michael Nielsen treats remembering as a choice rather than something left to chance. He uses Anki, a spaced-repetition program that brings a fact back just before you would forget it, and argues its real value is understanding difficult material in depth, reading a paper or book closely enough that its ideas stay with you, rather than the rote drilling the tool is usually associated with. His case is that memory of this kind sits close to the center of problem-solving and creativity, since it's hard to think fluently in a subject whose basics keep slipping away. You might start a small deck for one paper or idea you keep half-forgetting, and spend a few minutes with it each day this week.
