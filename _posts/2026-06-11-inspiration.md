### A bat 'fact' that went 80 years untested

For more than 80 years, textbooks repeated that bats do not move their eyes, a claim that traces to a single confident assertion by the optometrist Gordon Walls and was never once checked with measurements. When Kathleen Cullen's lab at Johns Hopkins finally measured it, Seba's short-tailed bat tracked moving patterns with smooth eye movements spanning roughly ten degrees in each direction, the first hard evidence that bats steer their gaze the way other mammals do. The lasting part is not the bats themselves but how long a tidy claim can pass for knowledge on borrowed authority alone.

### One organism the size of a town

Under the Malheur National Forest in eastern Oregon, a single honey fungus, Armillaria ostoyae, spreads across 2,385 acres, close to four square miles, almost all of it hidden as black, shoestring filaments threading through the soil from one tree root to the next. Genetic testing shows it is one individual rather than a colony, with estimates putting its weight near 35,000 tons and its age in the thousands of years. Most of the time it shows nothing above ground; in autumn it sends up clusters of honey mushrooms, the only visible sign of the largest known single living thing on Earth.

### Imagine the plan already failed

The psychologist Gary Klein suggests a move he calls the premortem: before a group commits to a plan, it pauses, imagines that a year has passed and the plan has failed badly, and then each person spends five to ten minutes writing the history of exactly how it went wrong. Daniel Kahneman features it in Thinking, Fast and Slow because naming a future failure out loud gives people room to raise doubts that optimism and group consensus usually keep quiet. Before your next real decision, you might write that short history of the disaster first and notice which risks only become sayable once you have assumed the failure already happened.
