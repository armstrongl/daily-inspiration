### Trees are a strategy, not a lineage

A maple and a mulberry are both trees, yet their most recent common ancestor was not one. Writing in "There's no such thing as a tree," Georgia Ray works through the phylogenetics: a tree is not a branch on the evolutionary tree, it is a body plan that unrelated plant lineages have independently invented at least nine separate times, much as ocean arthropods keep re-evolving into crabs. Wood and height were never handed down from a single ancestral tree; they are a recurring solution that distant relatives keep reinventing on their own.

### The flood that refilled the Mediterranean

Around 5.3 million years ago the Mediterranean had nearly dried to a salt-crusted basin lying far below the level of the world ocean. Then the Atlantic breached the rock sill at Gibraltar. Reconstructing the event from borehole and seismic data, Daniel Garcia-Castellanos and colleagues estimate the inflow cut a channel some 200 kilometres long and ran at up to a thousand times the Amazon's flow, raising the sea by more than ten metres a day and refilling most of the basin in a span of months to roughly two years.

### A checklist is for the expert

A reframe grounded in Atul Gawande's "The Checklist": we tend to treat a checklist as a crutch for the careless, but Gawande reports the reverse. When the critical-care physician Peter Pronovost had Michigan intensive-care units adopt a five-step checklist for inserting a central line, a procedure their doctors had each done thousands of times, infection rates that had seemed irreducible fell toward zero. The point was not that the doctors lacked skill; it was that under load, even an expert quietly skips steps they know by heart. You might pick one task you think you are too practiced to need help with, write down its five most forgettable steps, and notice what your hands have been dropping.
