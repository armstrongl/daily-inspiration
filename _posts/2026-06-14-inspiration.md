### Cockroaches that mate for life

The wood-feeding cockroach *Salganea taiwanensis* appears to form lifelong pair bonds, a behavior almost unknown in invertebrates. After burrowing into rotting wood, a male and female spend hours eating each other's wings. In experiments by Nate Lo (University of Sydney) and Haruka Osaki, wing-eaten pairs rammed and body-slammed any outsider who entered their nest while remaining tolerant of each other; unbonded pairs let visitors walk right in.

### Homer in the wrappings

At the site of ancient Oxyrhynchus in Egypt's Minya province, a Spanish-Egyptian archaeological team uncovered Roman-era mummies fitted with golden tongues, objects placed in the mouths of the dead to ensure they could speak in the afterlife. Inside one of the mummies, the team found a papyrus containing a passage from Book II of Homer's *Iliad*, the Catalogue of Ships listing the Greek forces at Troy. It is the first known instance of a Greek literary text deliberately included in the embalming process. Someone two thousand years ago chose which lines of Homer to carry into the ground.

*Chosen because the deliberate inclusion of Homer in a mummy's wrappings turns a routine archaeological report into something that reaches across two millennia of human relationship with literature.*

### Ask what you'd tell a friend

Psychologist Igor Grossmann and Ethan Kross identified what they call Solomon's Paradox: across three experiments with 693 participants, people consistently reasoned more wisely about a friend's relationship problem than about their own. The asymmetry disappeared when participants adopted a third-person perspective on their own situation. The next time you're facing a personal dilemma, you might try a version of their technique: describe the situation as if a friend were telling it to you, then give that friend your honest advice.

[Read the source](https://doi.org/10.1177/0956797614535400)

*Chosen because the technique is immediately testable, the paradox names a specific cognitive asymmetry most people can recognize in themselves, and the next step is a single sentence away.*
