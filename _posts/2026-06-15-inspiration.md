### A houseplant solving computational geometry

The Chinese money plant on your windowsill is solving a problem from computational geometry. Researchers at Cold Spring Harbor Laboratory mapped the vein networks on its round leaves and found they form Voronoi diagrams, the same spatial partitioning used in city planning and network design, with water-secreting pores as the seed points and looping veins as the boundaries. Across 34 leaves grown under shade, intense light, and high heat, the pattern held every time, ruling out a fixed genetic blueprint. The plant arrived at the answer without measuring anything.

### A lost rainforest recorded in rust

Beneath dry farmland in New South Wales lies one of the most detailed windows into deep time ever found. At a site called McGraths Flat, microscopic iron particles infiltrated the cells of organisms that died 11 to 16 million years ago in what was then a thriving rainforest, preserving individual pigment cells in fish eyes, internal organs of insects, and the fine hairs and neuronal tissues of spiders. The preservation medium is goethite, an iron-rich mineral previously thought incapable of this kind of detail. The region that is now dust was once canopy, and the iron in the soil recorded it at the cellular level.

*Chosen because the jump from "dry Australian farmland" to "individual pigment cells in a fish eye from 11 million years ago" produces a scale of temporal vastness that resists easy processing.*

### The two questions that unstick problems

When you're stuck on a problem, the obstacle might be the labels you're using. Psychologist Tony McCaffrey found that "functional fixedness," the tendency to see objects only by their common use, blocks creative solutions, and developed a two-question decomposition: for each part, ask "Can this be broken further?" and "Does my description imply a use?" People trained in this technique solved problems 67% more often. The next time something feels intractable, you might try describing each component stripped of its function words and see what opens up.

[Read the source](https://www.psychologicalscience.org/news/releases/stumped-by-a-problem-this-technique-unsticks-you.html)

*Chosen because the technique is specific enough to try on the next problem you encounter, and the mechanism (labels as blinders) is falsifiable and immediately recognizable.*
