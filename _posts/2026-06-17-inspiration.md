### A pigeon's compass is in its liver

For decades, scientists assumed pigeons navigate using light-sensitive molecules in their eyes or magnetic particles in their beaks. A study published in Science this May found the answer in the liver. Immune cells called macrophages, which break down old red blood cells, accumulate iron that makes them superparamagnetic and responsive to Earth's magnetic field. When researchers removed these cells, pigeons could still navigate home on sunny days but lost their way completely under overcast skies.

### Young sperm whales confirmed headbutting

Nineteenth-century sailors insisted that sperm whales rammed things with their heads. Melville built Moby Dick around the claim. Scientists noted it, debated it, but never confirmed it on camera. In March 2026, drone footage from the Azores captured the first documented instances of sperm whales striking each other head-on. The researchers expected large adult males competing for mates. Instead, it was younger whales, and whether they're playing, practicing, or fighting remains an open question.

*Chosen because a behavior woven into one of the great novels and reported by sailors for two centuries was finally caught on film, and the answer overturned assumptions about who does it and why.*

### Scientists who practice crafts file more patents

A PNAS study tracked STEMM professionals who maintained arts, crafts, or design hobbies alongside their scientific work. Those who practiced crafts across their careers filed and licensed patents at significantly higher rates than those who didn't. The researchers found that skills like visualizing, abstracting, and patterning transferred across domains, building denser bridges between unrelated ideas. If there's a craft you used to practice or one you've been curious about, the evidence suggests the time spent there feeds directly back into whatever your primary work is.
