### Most grammar universals don't survive testing

An international team tested 191 proposed grammar rules against data from 1,700 languages, using Bayesian methods that account for shared ancestry and geography. About two-thirds collapsed. The third that survived appear to persist not because they're hardwired into the brain, as Chomsky's framework assumed, but because of how languages change over time. The finding reframes universals as products of linguistic evolution rather than biological architecture.

[Read the source](https://www.mpg.de/25723124/enduring-patterns-in-the-world-s-languages)

*Chosen because it breaks both sides of a long-standing debate: neither "all languages share deep rules" nor "languages are arbitrary" turns out to be right, and the actual answer (evolutionary change creates recurring patterns) is more interesting than either.*

### Only the second baby solar system ever seen

Astronomers have confirmed only the second baby solar system ever observed: a star 437 light-years away with gas giants still forming from its protoplanetary disk. The first, PDS 70, was found in 2018. Until now, planet formation was a theoretical process inferred from end states. As Northwestern astronomer Jason Wang put it, "In astronomy, when we have a sample size of one, we have an anomaly. When we have a sample size of two, we have a population."

[Read the source](https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/astronomers-witness-the-birth-of-a-new-solar-system/)

*Chosen because seeing planets form in real time, rather than inferring formation from finished solar systems, marks a shift from theory to observation at a scale that makes 437 light-years feel close.*

### Birding physically reshapes the brain

A Journal of Neuroscience study this February scanned the brains of 58 adults and found that expert birders had physically denser tissue in regions controlling attention, perception, and working memory, with the differences persisting into older adulthood. Lead researcher Erik Wing noted that birding uniquely combines fine-grained identification, pattern detection, and constant comparison against internal templates. If you've been curious about picking up a field guide, the practice of learning to notice what's already outside your window appears to reshape the neural hardware you notice with.

[Read the source](https://www.jneurosci.org/content/early/2026/02/11/JNEUROSCI.1307-25.2026)

*Chosen because it converts "birdwatching is relaxing" into a concrete neuroscience finding about structural brain change, and the entry barrier is a window and curiosity.*
