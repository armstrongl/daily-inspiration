### Six events confirm an 87-year prediction

In 1939, physicist Arkady Migdal predicted that a recoiling atomic nucleus would drag its electron cloud behind it, ejecting electrons in the process. Nobody could observe it directly until a team at the University of the Chinese Academy of Sciences filtered six candidate events from nearly a million recorded collisions, each showing paired tracks of a recoiling nucleus and its shed electron. The confirmation, 87 years after the prediction, opens a new strategy for detecting light dark matter particles that current instruments cannot see.

*Chosen because an 87-year wait for direct observation, resolved by finding six signals in a million, is a concrete reminder of how long the gap between prediction and proof can stretch.*

### What grows beneath a 2,400-year-old tree

In Chile's Alerce Costero National Park, researchers sampled the soil beneath a 2,400-year-old alerce tree and found it hosts 2.25 times more fungal diversity than younger trees of the same species, including 361 fungal DNA sequences found nowhere else in the forest. These underground fungal networks supply the tree with water and nutrients while connecting it to surrounding plants, making the ancient tree an umbrella for the biodiversity of the entire forest floor. A millennium of environmental changes has assembled a fungal community beneath this one tree that no amount of replanting could reproduce.

*Chosen because the finding that what makes old-growth trees irreplaceable is mostly invisible, hidden in the soil rather than the canopy, reframes what "ancient" means in a forest.*

### The failed control that worked

Cambridge PhD student David Vahey was testing a photocatalyst when he ran a control experiment without it and found the reaction worked just as well, sometimes better. Instead of discarding what looked like a mistake, he investigated, and the team discovered a new light-powered method for modifying drug molecules late in development, skipping months of rebuilding compounds from scratch. If something has recently failed for you in a way that does not quite make sense, the pattern here might reframe whether it is worth a second look.

*Chosen because it documents a specific, replicable move: treating a failed control as a signal rather than noise, which produced a Nature Synthesis paper and a new class of reaction.*
