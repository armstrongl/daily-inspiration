### Printed neurons that talk to living brain cells

Northwestern engineers printed artificial neurons from semiconductor ink on flexible plastic. The voltage spikes these printed devices produce match the timing, shape, and duration of real action potentials closely enough that when placed against slices of mouse brain tissue, living Purkinje neurons fired back as though the signal came from a neighbor. The boundary between electronic and biological neural signaling turns out to be thinner than the devices themselves.

### Stars a million times the Sun hiding as red dots

Since JWST started mapping the early universe, astronomers have argued over hundreds of compact, red objects at extreme distances, nicknamed "little red dots," whose properties didn't match any known category. A Harvard team has now shown they are supermassive stars, each about a million times the mass of the Sun, so vast that their outer layers spread thin enough to glow red despite thermonuclear cores. These stars may be the direct precursors of the universe's first supermassive black holes.

[Read the source](https://iopscience.iop.org/article/10.3847/1538-4357/ae32f3)

*Chosen because a two-year mystery in early-universe astronomy resolves into single stars of almost incomprehensible mass, each one a black hole waiting to happen.*

### The repair that mattered more than the technique

Sociologist Allison Pugh spent years studying therapists, teachers, and physicians to understand what makes human connection work. The pivotal moment she documents: a therapist sensed something was off at the end of a session and called the patient back to name it. The patient, a trauma survivor, later said it was the first time anyone had noticed enough to say something. The next time a conversation feels slightly different from what you expected, it might be worth pausing to ask what shifted.
