### Your hippocampus listens without you

Neurosurgeon Vigi Katlowitz and colleagues at Baylor College of Medicine threaded Neuropixels probes into the hippocampi of seven epilepsy patients during surgery and played podcast episodes while the patients were fully unconscious under propofol. The hippocampus not only processed the incoming language in real time, distinguishing nouns from verbs and adjectives, but generated predictions about the next word in each sentence, a form of anticipatory coding previously assumed to require waking awareness. The finding, published in Nature, doesn't prove patients can "hear" during surgery, but it does collapse a tidy boundary: the one between what the brain can do with consciousness and what it can apparently do without it.

[Read the source](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-026-10448-0)

*Chosen because it dissolves the boundary between conscious and unconscious cognition with a single experiment: neurons predicting words in a brain that cannot report hearing them.*

### The impossible earthquake 90 kilometers down

In 1979, George Zandt, a postdoctoral seismologist at the University of Utah, analyzed recordings from a magnitude 3.8 earthquake near the town of Randolph and placed its origin at 90 kilometers underground, deep inside the Earth's mantle, where rock at 700°C is supposed to flow like taffy over millions of years, not fracture. No one believed him. Forty-seven years later, Zandt came out of retirement to co-author the paper that confirmed his finding and identified an entire class of "continental mantle earthquakes" occurring along the edge of the Wyoming Craton, an ancient slab of lithosphere that extends into the mantle like the keel of an iceberg, diverting the slow currents around it.

[Read the source](https://doi.org/10.1785/0320260006)

*Chosen because a postdoc's 47-year-old measurement, finally confirmed, reveals that the Earth fractures where it should only flow.*

### What craft teaches your day job

In a survey of over 6,000 STEMM professionals, Root-Bernstein et al. found that those who practiced arts, crafts, or design reported specific cognitive transfers to their scientific work: a furniture builder who said designing in wood taught him to "devise creative solutions to experimental problems," a biologist who credited sewing with training her to think in three-dimensional assembly, a data scientist who used her drawing skills to "bring out patterns from complex datasets." The transfers weren't vague claims about creativity; they named specific skills (spatial reasoning, manual dexterity, pattern recognition) acquired through specific crafts. If there's a craft or making practice you've let go dormant, it might be worth picking it back up and noticing what it teaches your other work.

[Read the source](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6369768/)

*Chosen because it converts a 6,000-person survey into a specific, testable claim: that making things with your hands changes how you think in your day job, and names the specific skills that transfer.*
