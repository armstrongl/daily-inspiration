### Severed tissue that refuses to die

Sara Jobson and colleagues at Memorial University amputated tube feet and tentacles from a scarlet sea cucumber (Psolus fabricii) and left them in a tank of flowing, unfiltered seawater. Three years later, the tissue is still alive: healing wounds, dividing cells, reorganizing its structure, and absorbing dissolved amino acids directly from the water. No sterile lab, no growth medium, no body. When the team tested tissue from sea stars, sea urchins, and other sea cucumber species, none survived more than three and a half months.

[Read the source](https://doi.org/10.1126/sciadv.aeb1394)

*Chosen because it breaks the assumption that tissue needs a body to stay alive, and does so in the dirtiest possible conditions: raw, unfiltered seawater.*

### A planet where dawn and dusk are different worlds

Cyril Gapp at the Max Planck Institute for Astronomy pointed JWST at the ultra-hot gas giant WASP-121 b and found that its dawn and dusk are two different atmospheres. Fierce eastward winds carry heat from the permanent dayside, making the evening terminator hotter and more expanded than the morning, with a temperature gradient of roughly 1,775 degrees Celsius across the divide. On the cooler side, water molecules survive long enough to absorb starlight; on the hotter side, they shatter apart. Where the temperature drops just enough, rock vapor may condense into silicate mineral clouds.

[Read the source](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41550-026-02887-6)

*Chosen because it makes the familiar concepts of dawn and dusk alien: on this planet, they are not times of day but permanently different chemical regimes separated by 1,775 degrees.*

### What happens when you look for 15 minutes instead of 30 seconds

At Philadelphia's Penn Museum, neuroscientist Anjan Chatterjee's team asked visitors to spend 15 minutes with a single artwork in three five-minute stages: sensory features, emotional response, then meaning. Liking barely changed, but perceived beauty increased significantly, and visitors reported more compassion, immersion, and a sense of having learned something. The typical museum visit gives an artwork less than 30 seconds. If there is something you pass every day that you have stopped actually seeing, what happens when you give it three unhurried minutes instead?

[Read the source](https://doi.org/10.1080/17439760.2025.2552793)

*Chosen because it shows that beauty is not instant recognition but something that emerges from sustained attention, and the experiment is one anyone can replicate tomorrow.*
