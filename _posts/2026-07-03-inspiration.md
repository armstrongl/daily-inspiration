### Your cells trade DNA with their neighbors

Your genome is not sealed inside your cells the way textbooks describe. A study published in Cell this year found that human cells can pass chromosome-sized pieces of DNA to each other through thin bridges called nanotubes, and the transferred DNA stays functional in its new home, getting transcribed and translated into protein. The researchers tagged DNA packaging proteins with different fluorescent colors, stressed the cells to break chromosomes, and filmed one color traveling down a nanotube into a cell of the other color. The finding raises an uncomfortable possibility: genomically unstable cancer cells might be able to hand their mutations to healthy neighbors.

*Chosen because it overturns the basic assumption that each cell's DNA stays sealed in its own nucleus, and the cancer implication makes the stakes concrete.*

### A cell built from dead ingredients

Kate Adamala's lab at the University of Minnesota packed nonliving molecules into a lipid membrane, piece by piece, until the resulting blob started behaving like life. Their creation, nicknamed SpudCell, grows, copies its DNA, and divides into daughter cells, all from roughly 150 to 200 molecular components. It is not alive by any definition. It cannot survive without being fed. But as Adamala put it: "We built a Wright flyer. The first bike frame with wings that flies 100 feet."

[Read the source](https://www.quantamagazine.org/for-the-first-time-a-cell-built-from-scratch-grows-and-divides-20260701/)

*Chosen because it is the strongest demonstration yet that nonliving chemistry can cross toward biology, and the honest framing ("it's a blob") keeps the wonder grounded.*

### Reading reshapes how you see faces

Neuroscientists long assumed that because reading is an evolutionary newcomer, the brain must steal visual processing space from older systems like face recognition. Falk Huettig at the Max Planck Institute tested this by comparing literate and illiterate adults in India and found the opposite: learning to read sharpened face recognition instead of degrading it. If you have been meaning to pick up a physical book but keep defaulting to scrolling, here is a reason that has nothing to do with relaxation: print readers consistently invest more cognitive effort than screen readers, and the brain rewards the difference.

*Chosen because it overturns an established neural theory with field data, and the action it invites (pick up a book) costs nothing and starts tonight.*
