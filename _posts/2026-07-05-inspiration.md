### Local measurements don't determine global shape

For 150 years, differential geometers assumed that knowing the distances and curvature at every point on a closed surface was enough to identify it uniquely. A team from TUM, TU Berlin, and NC State just disproved this by constructing two compact torus surfaces with identical local measurements that are nonetheless structurally different. Measuring everything about the parts, perfectly, still leaves the whole underdetermined.

### A shy cowherd's poem survived 1,300 years in Rome

Caedmon was a farm laborer at Whitby Abbey around 670 CE, too shy to recite poetry at feasts. According to Bede, he composed the first known English poem after a figure in a dream told him to sing about Creation. In May 2026, two Trinity College Dublin researchers found a previously unknown 9th-century copy of his nine-line hymn embedded directly in a Latin manuscript at Rome's National Central Library. The manuscript had been considered lost since 1975 after being stolen during the Napoleonic Wars, and resurfaced only because the library digitized its holdings and made them freely available.

### Habits click on, they don't fade in

A Johns Hopkins study in Nature Communications found that habits don't form through the gradual reinforcement psychology has assumed for a century. Instead, the brain appears to flip abruptly from deliberate choice to automatic behavior, and the researchers observed the reverse too: subjects acting habitually sometimes spontaneously returned to goal-directed behavior. If you're building a new routine and wondering when it will feel automatic, consider that the shift might be a sudden click rather than a slow fade, and that noticing which actions still feel deliberate might tell you more than counting streak days.

[Read the source](https://scitechdaily.com/surprising-new-study-challenges-a-century-old-theory-of-habit-formation/)

*Chosen because it replaces vague "keep at it" advice with a specific, testable mechanism that changes how you'd approach any behavior you're trying to build or break.*
