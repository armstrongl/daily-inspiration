### Scattered stones, coherent city

Ancient settlement sites frustrate archaeologists because, from the ground, collapsed walls and eroded structures look like random piles of stone. A University of Haifa team trained machine learning on drone imagery from nine sites across Israel, and the system identified 350,000 individual building stones. What looked like chaos from ground level turned out to be coherent architectural plans, with neighborhoods, construction phases, and spatial organization that no amount of standing and squinting would have revealed.

[Read the source](https://www.jpost.com/archaeology/article-880663)

*Chosen because it shows that disorder is sometimes just a resolution problem, not a property of what you're looking at.*

### A shrub survived 58 years of being extinct

Ptilotus senarius is a delicate shrub with purple-pink flowers that look like feathered fireworks. It hadn't been documented since 1967 and was considered extinct. Then Aaron Bean, a bird bander working on a remote Queensland property, photographed a strange plant and uploaded it to iNaturalist. Among millions of observations on the platform, the photo reached Anthony Bean, the botanist who had formally described the species a decade earlier. The plant was reclassified from extinct to critically endangered, which means it can now actually be protected.

[Read the source](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/05/260517211447.htm)

*Chosen because the whole chain, from one person's curiosity to a species getting a second chance, happened through a single uploaded photo.*

### Follow-up questions outperform all others

Across three studies of live conversations, Harvard researchers found that people who ask more follow-up questions are consistently better liked by their partners. Follow-up questions outperformed both topic-switching ("Speaking of...") and mirror questions (repeating what someone just said). The mechanism is specific: a follow-up signals "I heard what you said and I want to know more." In your next conversation today, consider replacing one topic change with a follow-up about whatever the other person just told you.

[Read the source](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28447835/)

*Chosen because it identifies the specific question type that works, not just that asking questions matters.*
