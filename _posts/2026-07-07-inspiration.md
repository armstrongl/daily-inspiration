### CRISPR's ancestor was hiding in viruses

Jennifer Doudna's lab used AlphaFold to search for proteins that structurally resemble CRISPR components but are encoded by unrelated genes, and found one lurking in bacteriophages. The system, called VIPR, predates CRISPR, silences genes by clamping onto DNA without cutting it, and reads its target through a gapped code that skips every third base pair. At roughly an eighth the size of Cas9, the entire system fits into a single gene therapy vector with room to spare.

*Chosen because it reverses the assumed direction of adaptive immunity's origins: the ancestor of our best gene-editing tool was a weapon viruses built to fight each other.*

### One mountain lion rewired a suburban preserve

When mountain lions started appearing on trail cameras at Stanford's Jasper Ridge Biological Preserve, a 485-hectare site 45 miles south of San Francisco, deer changed how they moved and foraged. Over 17 years, woody plant density, including young oaks, jumped 64-fold. The mechanism was not predation but the ecology of fear: deer simply ate less where they sensed a large cat had been, and the forest grew back.

*Chosen because a single visiting predator restoring an ecosystem in a suburban preserve makes conservation feel tractable at a scale most people can see from their window.*

### Reading upgrades visual systems it shouldn't touch

Neuroscientists long assumed that because reading is an evolutionary newcomer, the brain must cannibalize older visual networks, including face recognition, to make room for it. Falk Huettig's research at Max Planck, comparing literate and illiterate adults in India, found the opposite: literate adults were measurably better at recognizing faces. Reading fine-tunes the entire visual system rather than crowding it out. Next time you open a book, it might be worth noticing that the act is reshaping visual machinery that has nothing to do with text.
