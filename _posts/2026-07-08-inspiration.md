### Your brain learns from timing, not repetition

For a century, scientists assumed Pavlov's dog learned through repetition: more pairings of bell and food meant stronger learning. A UCSF team tested this by varying the spacing between trials in mice and found that animals receiving very few rewards learned just as much as those getting 20 times more, so long as the elapsed time was the same. The Nature Neuroscience paper shows that dopamine-driven learning rates scale with the duration between rewards, not the number of exposures.

### 110 unnamed species from a million square kilometers

Scientists aboard CSIRO's research vessel Investigator sampled between 200 and 3,000 meters deep in the Coral Sea Marine Park, nearly a million square kilometers of mostly unexplored water east of the Great Barrier Reef. They brought back more than 110 species new to science, including two rays, a deepwater catshark, a chimaera, and dozens of invertebrates from brittlestars to sea anemones. The count is expected to exceed 200 as taxonomists continue identifying cryptic specimens.

### Meditation produces sleep-like brain cleaning while awake

A PNAS study from Vanderbilt used MRI to show that focused attention meditation changes how cerebrospinal fluid moves through the brain, producing flow patterns similar to sleep, when the brain's glymphatic waste-clearing system is most active. The researchers found that meditation reduced regurgitant CSF flow through the cerebral aqueduct, a shift directionally opposite to what happens in aging and neurodegeneration. If you've been curious about meditation but put off by vague wellness framing, this is one specific, measurable mechanism that might be worth a 10-minute experiment.

[Read the source](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2504961122)
