### Your hair is pulled, not pushed

Textbooks have taught for decades that hair grows because dividing cells at the root push it upward. Researchers at Queen Mary University of London found the opposite: coordinated cells in the outer root sheath generate a pulling force that draws the hair up. When they blocked cell division, growth barely changed; when they blocked actin, the protein that lets cells move and contract, growth dropped more than 80%.

[Read the source](http://dx.doi.org/10.1038/s41467-025-65143-x)

*Chosen because it overturns a century-old textbook explanation with a clean experiment: blocking the assumed mechanism barely mattered, while blocking the unsuspected one shut growth down.*

### Mangrove forests are quietly coming back

A study published in Science, based on four decades of satellite data, finds that global mangrove forests are no longer in net decline. After losing nearly 2,900 square kilometers between the 1980s and 2010, gains have outpaced losses for the past 16 years, driven largely by mangroves recolonizing abandoned aquaculture ponds and expanding into newly formed river delta mudflats on their own.

[Read the source](https://www.plantsci.cam.ac.uk/news/global-mangrove-forests-recovering-offering-hopeful-sign-climate-and-coastal-resilience)

*Chosen because the recovery is driven largely by mangroves growing back on their own once the pressure stops, making the finding feel earned rather than engineered.*

### Free creativity runs on pleasure, not evaluation

Researchers at Aarhus University scanned the brains of 16 jazz pianists under three conditions and found that increasing creative freedom shifted activity away from evaluative networks and toward sensory, motor, and pleasure-related ones. The freer the improvisation, the less the brain judged and the more it sensed and enjoyed. If you're grinding on a creative problem and the ideas have gone flat, it might be worth dropping the constraints for a few minutes and noticing what comes when you stop evaluating.

[Read the source](https://doi.org/10.1111/nyas.70042)

*Chosen because it names a specific, measurable mechanism behind the common advice to "stop overthinking it," and the experiment is simple enough to approximate in any creative domain.*
