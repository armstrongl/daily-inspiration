### Cherry tomatoes contain 3,977 compounds

Nutrition science tracks about 150 chemicals in food: the macronutrients, vitamins, and minerals on a label. David Wishart's FooDB project at the University of Alberta has catalogued over 70,000 compounds actually present in food, including 40,000 previously unknown ones, and estimates that an average food item contains 20,000 to 50,000 individual compounds. Most of this "nutritional dark matter," a term coined by network scientist Albert-Laszlo Barabasi, has never been studied for health effects.

### A city of seven ravines, hidden on the steppe

For 20 years after its discovery, nobody had the resources to properly survey Semiyarka, a site on the Irtysh River in northeastern Kazakhstan. When a UCL-led team finally did, they found a 350-acre planned settlement from around 1600 BCE with evidence of industrial tin bronze production, monumental buildings, and a formal layout. "This site breaks from all the things that we thought we knew about Central Asia," says co-author Dan Lawrence: mobile communities built and sustained an organized urban hub on the open steppe.

*Chosen because it upends the assumed link between nomadic culture and impermanence with evidence from a single site.*

### The cost of searching for the best option

Barry Schwartz and colleagues at Swarthmore found that "maximizers," people who search exhaustively for the best possible option, report less satisfaction with their decisions, more regret, and lower happiness than "satisficers," who accept the first option that clears a personal threshold. Counterintuitively, adding more options makes maximizers feel worse, not better. The next time you catch yourself in an open-ended search, you might try naming what "good enough" looks like before you begin.

[Read the source](https://bschwartz.domains.swarthmore.edu/maximizing.pdf)

*Chosen because it names a daily habit most knowledge workers default to without noticing and offers a one-sentence alternative you can test today.*
