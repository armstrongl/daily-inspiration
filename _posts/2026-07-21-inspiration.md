### Your sensory cortex is already deciding

The textbook model of the brain works like a pipeline: sensory cortex collects raw data, passes it up to association areas, and decisions happen at the top. Yurii Vlasov's lab at the University of Illinois recorded neurons in mice navigating a virtual corridor and found decision-related signals firing in primary somatosensory cortex, the brain's very first relay station, driven by rapid feedback from higher areas. The architecture isn't a pipeline at all. Even the earliest cortical regions are already participating in what to do next.

### A star that ate its own planet

TOI-5882 is a sun-like star 1,300 light-years from Earth. Astronomer Brooke Kotten at the University of Michigan found it contains far more lithium than any star of its type should, the chemical fingerprint of a planet it swallowed. The missing world was somewhere between a few Earth masses and the mass of Neptune, likely pushed to its death by a nearby brown dwarf companion more than 20 times the size of Jupiter. Our own sun will do the same to Mercury, Venus, and possibly Earth in roughly 5 billion years.

### The constraint that turned out to be optional

For 50 years, manufacturing synthetic DNA required toxic chemical solvents, a limitation everyone optimized around but nobody removed. Donhee Ham's team at Harvard built a silicon chip that writes 64 DNA sequences simultaneously using only water-based enzymes and controlled electrical currents, published in Nature Electronics. The previous enzymatic record was about a dozen sequences. What assumption in your own process have you been working around rather than questioning whether it's necessary at all?
