### Your hair is pulled, not pushed

Textbooks have taught for decades that hair grows because dividing cells at the root push it upward. Researchers at Queen Mary University of London found the opposite: coordinated cells in the outer root sheath generate a pulling force that draws the hair up. When they blocked cell division, growth barely changed; when they blocked actin, the protein that lets cells move and contract, growth dropped more than 80%.

[Read the source](http://dx.doi.org/10.1038/s41467-025-65143-x)

*Chosen because it overturns a century-old textbook explanation with a clean experiment: blocking the assumed mechanism barely mattered, while blocking the unsuspected one shut growth down.*

Most materials stiffen when hit hard. Rice does the opposite: it weakens under rapid compression but holds firm under slow pressure, because friction between grains drops sharply at high loading speeds, collapsing the internal force networks that carry the load. A University of Birmingham team published in Matter that they exploited this quirk to build a metamaterial that switches its stiffness automatically on impact, no electronics or sensors required.

UCSF researchers assigned older adults to take a weekly 15-minute "awe walk" for eight weeks, with the only instruction being to seek out things that felt vast or that they did not fully understand. ### Severed tissue that refuses to die

Sara Jobson and colleagues at Memorial University amputated tube feet and tentacles from a scarlet sea cucumber (Psolus fabricii) and left them in a tank of flowing, unfiltered seawater. Three years later, the tissue is still alive: healing wounds, dividing cells, reorganizing its structure, and absorbing dissolved amino acids directly from the water. No sterile lab, no growth medium, no body. When the team tested tissue from sea stars, sea urchins, and other sea cucumber species, none survived more than three and a half months.

[Read the source](https://doi.org/10.1126/sciadv.aeb1394)

*Chosen because it breaks the assumption that tissue needs a body to stay alive, and does so in the dirtiest possible conditions: raw, unfiltered seawater.*

### A planet where dawn and dusk are different worlds

Cyril Gapp at the Max Planck Institute for Astronomy pointed JWST at the ultra-hot gas giant WASP-121 b and found that its dawn and dusk are two different atmospheres. Fierce eastward winds carry heat from the permanent dayside, making the evening terminator hotter and more expanded than the morning, with a temperature gradient of roughly 1,775 degrees Celsius across the divide. On the cooler side, water molecules survive long enough to absorb starlight; on the hotter side, they shatter apart. Where the temperature drops just enough, rock vapor may condense into silicate mineral clouds.

[Read the source](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41550-026-02887-6)

*Chosen because it makes the familiar concepts of dawn and dusk alien: on this planet, they are not times of day but permanently different chemical regimes separated by 1,775 degrees.*

### Your hippocampus listens without you

Neurosurgeon Vigi Katlowitz and colleagues at Baylor College of Medicine threaded Neuropixels probes into the hippocampi of seven epilepsy patients during surgery and played podcast episodes while the patients were fully unconscious under propofol. The hippocampus not only processed the incoming language in real time, distinguishing nouns from verbs and adjectives, but generated predictions about the next word in each sentence, a form of anticipatory coding previously assumed to require waking awareness. The finding, published in Nature, doesn't prove patients can "hear" during surgery, but it does collapse a tidy boundary: the one between what the brain can do with consciousness and what it can apparently do without it.

[Read the source](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-026-10448-0)

*Chosen because it dissolves the boundary between conscious and unconscious cognition with a single experiment: neurons predicting words in a brain that cannot report hearing them.*

### The impossible earthquake 90 kilometers down

In 1979, George Zandt, a postdoctoral seismologist at the University of Utah, analyzed recordings from a magnitude 3.8 earthquake near the town of Randolph and placed its origin at 90 kilometers underground, deep inside the Earth's mantle, where rock at 700°C is supposed to flow like taffy over millions of years, not fracture. No one believed him. Forty-seven years later, Zandt came out of retirement to co-author the paper that confirmed his finding and identified an entire class of "continental mantle earthquakes" occurring along the edge of the Wyoming Craton, an ancient slab of lithosphere that extends into the mantle like the keel of an iceberg, diverting the slow currents around it.

[Read the source](https://doi.org/10.1785/0320260006)

*Chosen because a postdoc's 47-year-old measurement, finally confirmed, reveals that the Earth fractures where it should only flow.*
